# Community Intelligence Stage

## 1. Purpose

Community Intelligence is the first stage of the AI Editorial Operating System. It exists to discover editorial opportunities from real human discussions before any keyword research occurs.

The core belief is:

> We do not start from keywords.
> We start from people.

Keywords validate demand. They do not create editorial insight. A keyword can tell you how many people search for a phrase, but it cannot tell you why they search, what they actually want, what frustrates them, or why existing content fails them. Community Intelligence answers those questions.

### What Community Intelligence discovers

- Recurring questions
- Recurring frustrations
- Recurring misconceptions
- Recurring buying objections
- Recurring comparisons
- Emerging discussions
- Unsolved problems
- Emotional context behind searches

### What Community Intelligence does NOT do

- Keyword research
- Search volume analysis
- Competition analysis
- SERP analysis
- Trend data from keyword tools

Those happen later, in Opportunity Discovery. Community Intelligence creates the raw material. Opportunity Discovery validates, prioritises, and scores it.

---

## 2. Editorial Philosophy

### Start from people

Every article should answer a real question someone actually asked. Not a keyword a tool suggested. Not a topic a competitor ranks for. A question a human being posted in a community because they needed an answer.

When an article originates from a real community discussion:

- The intent is known (not inferred)
- The language is real (not optimised)
- The emotional weight is visible (not guessed)
- The content gap is proven (the question keeps getting asked because existing answers fail)

### Search demand validates; it does not create

Search volume is a gate, not a source. Before an opportunity reaches the Research Brief stage, it must pass through search validation. But the opportunity itself was born in a community discussion, not in a keyword tool.

This protects against a common failure mode: writing about something because it has search volume, even when no human actually needs the content.

### Keywords are not topics

A keyword like "best protein powder" is not a topic. It is a search query. The topic lives in the community discussion:

- *Why do people keep asking "is there a protein powder that doesn't bloat me?"*
- *Why do beginners ask "do I even need protein powder?"*
- *Why do people ask "which protein powder tastes good and mixes well?"*

Each of those questions contains an editorial opportunity. The keyword "best protein powder" is just the entry point for search validation.

## 3. Inputs

### Current sources

| Source | Type | Why |
|--------|------|-----|
| Reddit | Public forums | Highest density of raw, unfiltered questions and objections. Threads are organised by community. Upvotes indicate shared sentiment. |
| Quora | Q&A platform | Questions are structured and tagged. High signal-to-noise for specific informational gaps. |
| Niche forums | Topic-specific communities | Deeper discussion than Reddit. Higher expertise level. More detailed problem descriptions. |

### Planned sources

| Source | Type | Why |
|--------|------|-----|
| Facebook Groups | Private communities | Higher trust discussions. More vulnerable questions. Less performative than public platforms. |
| Discord servers | Real-time chat | Emerging topics appear faster. Conversations are candid and immediate. |
| YouTube comments | Video discussions | Comments reveal objections and questions that the video itself did not answer. High emotional intensity. |
| X (Twitter) | Micro-blogging | Trending discussions, hot takes, and real-time sentiment. Public figures drive conversations. |
| LinkedIn | Professional network | B2B and professional niche discussions. Higher signal quality for business-related topics. |
| Amazon/App Store reviews | Product reviews | Raw feedback at the point of purchase and use. Reveals what buyers wish they knew before buying. |
| Support tickets | Customer service | If accessible, the highest-signal source of unsolved problems. |

### What to extract from each source

| Signal | Example |
|--------|---------|
| Exact phrasing of questions | "Can I do affiliate marketing without a website?" |
| Emotional language | "I'm so frustrated that..." / "I wish someone had told me..." |
| Self-reported failures | "I tried X for 6 months and made nothing" |
| Objections to advice | "That won't work because..." |
| Comparison requests | "Which is better for beginners, X or Y?" |
| Misconceptions stated as fact | "Affiliate marketing is a pyramid scheme" |
| Unsolved problems | "Nobody has explained how to do X" |
| Conflicting advice | "One person says X, another says Y" |

## 4. The Full Workflow

```
Community Discovery
    ↓
Question Mining
    ↓
Problem Mining
    ↓
Root Cause Analysis
    ↓
Solution Gap Analysis
    ↓
Opportunity Mapping
    ↓
Editorial Planning
    ↓
Opportunity Discovery
    ↓
Research Brief
    ↓
Writer
    ↓
QA
    ↓
Publish
```

### Stage descriptions

#### 1. Community Discovery

Identify the communities where the target audience already gathers. For each niche, maintain a list of active subreddits, Quora spaces, forums, and other discussion hubs.

**Output:** Community list with engagement indicators.

**Criteria for a valuable community:**
- Regularly active (new posts daily)
- Questions recur (same topics appear repeatedly)
- Users are in the problem phase (seeking solutions, not just discussing)
- Moderation is light enough to allow candid discussion

#### 2. Question Mining

Extract recurring questions from community discussions. Questions are the most direct signal of an editorial opportunity. If the same question appears across multiple threads, multiple communities, and multiple time periods, there is unmet demand.

**What to capture:**
- The exact wording of the question
- How many times it appears
- Across how many communities
- The context in which it is asked (beginner, intermediate, advanced)
- Whether existing answers exist and why they are insufficient

**Distinguish between:**
- Information questions ("What is X?") — definitional gaps
- How-to questions ("How do I X?") — procedural gaps
- Decision questions ("Which X should I choose?") — comparison gaps
- Verification questions ("Is X legitimate?") — trust gaps
- Troubleshooting questions ("Why doesn't X work?") — implementation gaps

#### 3. Problem Mining

Move beyond questions to capture problems. Questions are what people ask. Problems are what people struggle with. A problem may not be directly articulated as a question. It may appear as a complaint, a frustration, a confession of failure, or a request for help.

**What to capture:**
- Explicit frustrations ("I've been trying X and nothing works")
- Self-reported failures ("I wasted 6 months on X")
- Emotional signals ("I'm so overwhelmed", "I feel like giving up")
- Implicit problems revealed through context
- The gap between what the person wants and what they have achieved

**Frequency indicators:**
- How many unique users express this problem
- How often it appears (daily, weekly, monthly)
- Whether it spans multiple communities
- Whether experienced users have an answer or everyone is guessing

#### 4. Root Cause Analysis

For each recurring problem, determine the root cause. A beginner who says "I wrote 50 articles and made no money" may have:

- A traffic problem (no one reads the articles)
- A conversion problem (people read but don't buy)
- A product problem (they promote poor offers)
- An audience problem (wrong niche)
- A trust problem (content is not persuasive)
- A timing problem (SEO takes 6+ months to work)

Root cause analysis separates the expression of the problem from the actual cause. This is critical because the editorial angle must address the root cause, not the symptom.

**Analysis framework:**

```
Problem expression: "I've been publishing for 6 months and made $0"
    ↓
What could cause this? → No traffic? → Bad keywords? → Low search volume? → Wrong niche?
                      → Poor conversion? → Wrong offers? → Weak content? → No trust?
                      → Technical issue? → Broken links? → Wrong tracking?
    ↓
Eliminate each possibility
    ↓
Arrive at root cause
    ↓
Is there an editorial opportunity to address this root cause?
```

#### 5. Solution Gap Analysis

For each problem, examine the existing solutions. Why hasn't the community's question been answered adequately?

**Existing content failure modes:**

| Failure | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Too generic | Answers the surface question but ignores the real context |
| Too advanced | Assumes knowledge the beginner does not have |
| Too promotional | Clearly an affiliate pitch, not genuine help |
| Outdated | Written before algorithm changes or new tools |
| Assumes resources | "Just hire an expert" or "use this paid tool" |
| No emotional validation | Dismisses the fear/frustration behind the question |
| Contradicts other advice | User hears "X works" and "X doesn't work" with no way to evaluate |

**The solution gap is the editorial opportunity.**

If existing content fails because it is too generic, the opportunity is to write something specific. If existing content fails because it assumes resources the beginner does not have, the opportunity is to write for zero-budget beginners.

#### 6. Opportunity Mapping

Map each identified problem to potential editorial formats.

| Problem type | Best format |
|-------------|-------------|
| "How do I start?" | Step-by-step guide |
| "Which is better?" | Comparison article |
| "Is X legitimate?" | Honest review or myth-busting |
| "Why doesn't X work?" | Troubleshooting guide |
| "I'm frustrated with X" | Empathy-driven educational content |
| "Nobody talks about X" | Insider knowledge article |
| "Everyone says X but I found Y" | Contrarian take or case study |

**Also map:**
- Related questions that could be grouped into a single article
- Questions that form a natural series
- Questions that reveal a misunderstanding (myth-busting opportunity)
- Questions that reveal a buying intent (review/comparison opportunity)

#### 7. Editorial Planning

Produce a list of recommended articles with:

- Working title
- Primary question it answers
- Root problem it addresses
- Target audience segment
- Recommended format
- Related questions to include
- Potential affiliate products to naturally integrate
- Internal linking candidates

## 5. Required Outputs

Each Community Intelligence Report must contain the following sections:

### 5.1 Executive Summary

A 2-3 paragraph overview of the most important findings from the community research. What is the single biggest editorial opportunity? What pattern emerged most strongly? What should the editorial team act on immediately?

### 5.2 Recurring Questions

The top questions that appear across communities, ranked by frequency and spread. Each entry includes:

- The exact question
- Number of occurrences observed
- Communities where it appears
- Whether good answers exist
- Why existing answers are insufficient

### 5.3 Recurring Problems

The top problems users express, separated from the questions. A problem may not be formulated as a question. Capture the emotional valence.

Each entry includes:

- How users describe the problem
- The emotional language used
- How many users express it
- Whether any solution exists in the community

### 5.4 Root Causes

For each recurring problem, the likely root cause. Not what users *say* the problem is — what the problem actually is beneath the surface.

### 5.5 Existing Content Failures

An analysis of why existing content does not solve each problem. Specific article titles or content types that are failing, and why.

### 5.6 Can Content Solve This?

A gate question. Not every problem can be solved by publishing an article. Some problems require tools, services, or behaviour change. Be honest about whether an article is the right solution.

### 5.7 Can OLSP Help?

An honest assessment of whether the OLSP platform or ecosystem can be positioned as part of the solution. If the answer is no, say no. Forcing an OLSP connection where none exists produces low-quality content.

### 5.8 Opportunity Score

A numeric score (1-10) for each identified opportunity based on:

| Factor | Weight |
|--------|--------|
| Question frequency | How often does this question appear? |
| Community spread | How many communities discuss it? |
| Existing content quality | How poorly is this currently served? |
| Emotional intensity | How much frustration/confusion exists? |
| Search validation potential | Is there likely search volume? (estimate, not data) |
| Content feasibility | Can we write something genuinely better? |
| OLSP alignment | Natural fit with OLSP positioning? |

### 5.9 Editorial Angles

3-5 distinct angles for each high-scoring opportunity. Different ways to frame the same topic for different audience segments.

### 5.10 Recommended Articles

A prioritised list of articles to produce, with:

- Working title
- Primary question
- Root problem
- Recommended format
- Estimated word count range
- Target audience
- Rationale for priority ranking

### 5.11 Cluster Recommendations

Groups of related articles that should be produced together as a content cluster. A single root problem may require 3-5 articles from different angles to comprehensively address it.

### 5.12 Community Source Log

For auditability, a log of every community discussion that informed the report, with links to the original threads.

## 6. Relationship with Opportunity Discovery

Community Intelligence and Opportunity Discovery are separate stages with distinct responsibilities.

### Community Intelligence (before)

**Role:** Source of editorial opportunities

- Discovers topics from real human discussions
- Captures exact language, emotions, and context
- Does not consider search volume, competition, or ranking potential
- Output is qualitative and raw

### Opportunity Discovery (after)

**Role:** Validator and prioritisation engine

- Validates that community-identified questions have search demand
- Scores opportunities by search volume, competition, and intent
- Clusters related opportunities into content groups
- Produces structured Opportunity Briefs
- Does not invent topics — it processes what Community Intelligence produces

### Why this ordering matters

When keyword research drives ideation, you get content that answers a search query but may not answer a real human question. The search volume exists, but the content does not actually help anyone. The article ranks, people click, they bounce, and nothing converts.

When community intelligence drives ideation, you get content that answers a real question a human actually asked. The search volume is validation that other people also have that question. The article ranks, people click, they stay, and they convert.

The ordering enforces a discipline:

> First, find out what people are actually struggling with.
> Then, check whether enough people search for it to be worth publishing.

Not the reverse.

## 7. How It Changes the Editorial Workflow

### Before Community Intelligence

```
Keyword Research → Topic Ideation → Content Brief → Writer → Publish
```

Problems with this workflow:
- Topics are derived from search data, not human conversations
- Content briefs lack emotional context
- Writers produce articles that answer keyword intent, not human confusion
- The resulting content is interchangeable with any other site targeting the same keyword

### After Community Intelligence

```
Community Discovery → Question Mining → Problem Mining → Root Cause Analysis →
Solution Gap Analysis → Opportunity Mapping → Editorial Planning →
Opportunity Discovery (validation) → Research Brief → Writer → QA → Publish
```

What changed:

| Aspect | Before | After |
|--------|--------|-------|
| Topic origin | Keyword tool suggestion | Community discussion |
| Writer receives | Keyword + outline | Real question + context + emotion + failed existing content |
| Content intent | Match search intent | Solve a human problem |
| Differentiation | SEO optimisation | Unique community insight |
| Conversion driver | Keyword matching | Trust built through demonstrated understanding |
| Risk | Promoting based on volume | Promoting based on actual demand |

### What the writer receives

A Research Brief that includes:
- The exact question from a real community discussion (with link)
- The emotional context around the question
- What existing content gets wrong
- What the root cause of the problem is
- The editorial angle (not just the keyword)
- Real quotes from community discussions to ground the writing
- Affiliate products that naturally fit the solution

This produces content that feels like it was written by someone who understands the audience — because it was.

## 8. Future Expansion

The following capabilities are reserved for future implementation. They are documented here for architectural awareness. No implementation work is planned.

### 8.1 Automated Community Crawling

Periodic automated collection of discussions from:
- Reddit (via subreddit feeds)
- Quora (via topic feeds)
- Niche forums (via RSS or scraping)

Collected data stored for analysis without real-time API dependencies.

### 8.2 Sentiment Analysis

Natural language processing to classify community posts by:
- Emotional valence (positive, negative, neutral)
- Emotional intensity (frustrated, curious, satisfied)
- Intent category (information, purchase, troubleshooting, comparison)

### 8.3 Discussion Clustering

Automated grouping of similar questions and problems across sources. A single root problem expressed in 50 different ways across 10 communities should be clustered into one opportunity.

Techniques reserved for future:
- Semantic similarity matching
- FAQ clustering algorithms
- Topic modelling

### 8.4 Trend Detection

Identification of:
- Rising question frequency (a problem growing in importance)
- New terminology (emerging concepts)
- Shifting sentiment (a previously accepted solution falling out of favour)
- Seasonal patterns (questions that recur on a schedule)

### 8.5 AI-Generated Opportunity Scoring

Machine learning model trained on historical performance data to predict:
- Likely search demand for community-identified questions (without keyword tools)
- Content format that performs best for each problem type
- Optimal word count range
- Conversion probability
